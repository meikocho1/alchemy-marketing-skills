# Costly Signaling — trust is bought with waste

People can't see your intentions, only your actions — so they read your *costly, hard-to-fake* actions as proof. Anything cheap and easy to fake (a slogan, a claim, a promise) carries little signal. Anything expensive, effortful, or risky to you carries a lot. This is why "wasteful" things work.

## The principle

A signal is credible in proportion to how much it would *cost a liar*. The peacock's tail is useless and metabolically expensive — that's exactly why it's a believable signal of fitness. Apply the same logic to a brand:

- **Advertising spend is itself the message.** A big, visible campaign says "we expect to be here long enough to recoup this, and we have repeat customers." A fly-by-night operator can't afford it. The customer doesn't need to *read* the ad to get the signal.
- **Guarantees, warranties, free returns** are costly signals: you'd only offer them if the product actually held up. The cost to you is the proof.
- **Over-delivery and effort** ("we'll answer in three rings," lavish packaging, a hand-written note) signal commitment because they obviously cost something.
- **Price can be the feature.** For Veblen goods (champagne, perfume, premium tools), a *higher* price increases desirability and even perceived performance — the expensive placebo genuinely works better. Cutting the price can destroy the signal.

## The Pratfall Effect (the honest-flaw signal)

Admitting a real weakness is a costly, hard-to-fake move — so it reads as honesty, and honesty raises trust in everything else you say.

- **Avis: "We're number two. We try harder."** Owning the weakness made the brand more likeable and more credible than claiming to be the best.
- "It's ugly, but it works." / "We're not the cheapest, but here's why." A small genuine flaw makes the rest believable. Perfection reads as marketing; an admitted flaw reads as a person.

## How to apply

1. **Read your current signal.** Forget what you *say* — what do your actions cost you, and therefore prove? Cheap claims ("trusted," "premium," "#1") prove nothing.
2. **Find a signal a liar couldn't afford.** A guarantee that would bankrupt you if the product were bad. Visible, sustained presence. Effort the customer can see. Real expertise on display.
3. **Consider price as a signal, not just a cost.** If trust/desire is the problem, a *higher* price plus a costlier-looking presentation can outperform a discount. Test before assuming cheaper wins.
4. **Add one honest flaw (Pratfall).** State a real limitation plainly. It buys credibility for every true claim around it. Never invent a fake flaw — that's just another cheap claim.
5. **Make sure the signal is honest.** A costly signal works *because* it's tied to reality (you really will honour the guarantee, you really are committed). Faking it is expensive to you and fatal when discovered.

## 日本語の使用例

- 「広告にお金をかける意味がわからない、クリックも測れない」→ 広告は**支出そのものがシグナル**。"これだけ出せる＝続く会社/リピートがある" と無意識に伝わる。直接効果だけで測ると本質を外す。
- 「強気の価格にすると売れなくなる気がする」→ 信頼・憧れが課題なら**値下げはシグナルを壊す**。高い価格＋それに見合う見せ方の方が効くことがある（ヴェブレン財）。counterintuitive-tests で検証。
- 「完璧に良く見せたいのに刺さらない」→ **プラットフォール効果**。本当の弱点を1つ正直に開示すると、残りの主張全部の信頼が上がる。
- 葬儀/シニア領域：「安心してもらいたい」→ "安心です" と言う（安い主張）のではなく、**追加料金ゼロの明朗会計・返金保証・24時間必ず人が出る** など "嘘つきには払えないコスト" を提示する。
